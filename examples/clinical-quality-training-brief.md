# Example: Clinical Quality Training Brief

Fictional and sanitized portfolio example. Not clinical advice.

## Training Topic

Improving consistency in behavioral health documentation for care management reviews.

## Audience

Licensed clinicians, clinical supervisors, quality reviewers, and operations partners.

## Problem Statement

Clinical teams often understand the member's situation but document it inconsistently. When documentation is inconsistent, it becomes harder to assess acuity, show rationale, identify risk, coach staff, and compare quality trends across reviewers.

## Learning Goals

By the end of the training, participants should be able to:

- Separate member-reported information from clinical interpretation
- Document risk and protective factors clearly
- Connect interventions to identified needs
- Use consistent language for follow-up planning
- Recognize when a case needs escalation or consultation

## Sample Scenario

A member reports increased anxiety, disrupted sleep, and missed workdays after a recent family stressor. The member denies current suicidal intent but reports feeling overwhelmed and isolated. The clinician provides coping-skills coaching, discusses supports, and schedules follow-up.

## Documentation Questions

1. What did the member report directly?
2. What clinical impressions are supported by the information available?
3. What risk factors are present?
4. What protective factors are present?
5. What intervention was provided?
6. What follow-up plan was established?
7. Does the note explain why the next step is appropriate?

## Before and After Example

### Less Useful

Member is anxious and needs follow-up. Discussed coping skills. No safety concerns.

### More Useful

Member reported increased anxiety, poor sleep, and missed workdays following a family stressor. Member denied current suicidal intent or plan. Risk factors include increased distress and social isolation. Protective factors include willingness to engage, future orientation, and agreement to follow-up. Clinician reviewed grounding strategies and helped member identify one support person to contact. Follow-up scheduled to reassess symptoms, coping use, and need for additional care.

## Quality Review Rubric

| Domain | Strong documentation shows |
| --- | --- |
| Presenting concern | Specific member-reported concerns and context |
| Risk | Current risk, protective factors, and escalation rationale |
| Intervention | What the clinician did, not just that support was provided |
| Plan | Clear next step and reason for that step |
| Consistency | Terms are understandable to another reviewer |

## Facilitation Notes

- Start with examples rather than policy language
- Ask clinicians what information they would need if they inherited the case tomorrow
- Normalize that good documentation is a clinical tool, not just an audit requirement
- Use calibration discussion to reduce variation across reviewers

## Why this example matters

Quality improvement works best when it respects clinicians and gives them practical tools. The goal is not more words. The goal is clearer clinical thinking that another person can safely follow.
